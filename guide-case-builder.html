WMS Case Builder

User Guide for Testing

⚠ Important — this is a prototype
This tool is in active development and has not yet been independently validated. Always apply your own professional judgment. Your feedback is what will improve it.

What this tool does

The WMS Case Builder helps you generate high-quality fictional teaching cases for classroom discussion, assignments, workshops, and exams. It guides you through a short interview to understand your teaching context, then generates three scenario options for you to choose from before building a full case.

The tool produces a first draft — not a finished case. You are expected to read, refine, and adapt the output before using it in teaching or assessment. You remain responsible for the accuracy, appropriateness, and pedagogical quality of any case you use.

Before you start

You do not need to prepare documents in advance. It helps to have a rough sense of:

  • The course or topic. e.g. Strategic Management, Leadership and Change, Marketing.
  • What you want students to do with the case. e.g. apply a specific framework, identify a dilemma, make a recommendation.
  • The case style. Highly Structured (exams, new students), Balanced (regular coursework), or Story-Driven (advanced students who must infer the problem).
  • Approximate length. Short (300–500 words), Medium (600–900 words), or Long (1,000–1,500 words).

Optionally, you can upload a reference case you consider high quality. The tool will calibrate its tone and style to match it.

How it works — step by step

  1. Open the tool. It will explain the process briefly, then begin the interview.
  2. Answer the interview questions. The tool asks up to 11 questions covering student level, topic, learning outcomes, case type, organisational context, central challenge, teaching use, student deliverable, length, data needs, and case style. You can answer as many or as few as you like. Type "generate now" at any point if you want it to proceed with what it has.
  3. Choose from three scenario options. The tool presents three distinct scenario outlines, each differing on at least two dimensions. Pick the one that best fits your teaching context.
  4. Receive the full case. The tool expands the chosen scenario into a complete teaching case and runs a quality check before outputting. Optionally ask for a teaching note — it includes a synopsis, discussion questions, theoretical connections, and common student errors.
  5. Review and adapt. Read the case carefully. Check that the dilemma is genuine, the context is plausible, and no stakeholder inadvertently signals the right answer. Revise before using in teaching.

What to watch for

  • Is the central dilemma genuinely open — could a well-prepared student argue for at least two different courses of action?
  • Does the case feel specific and plausible, or generic and vague?
  • Are the stakeholders real sources of tension, or just props?
  • Does the tone and complexity match the student level you specified?
  • For Story-Driven cases: is the problem never stated explicitly? Students should have to infer it.
  • Is there anything in the case that would be inappropriate or misleading for students?

How to give feedback on the tool

After testing, please note:

  • How well the generated case matched your teaching context
  • Whether the scenario options were genuinely distinct and useful
  • Quality of the dilemma — was it genuinely open or did it signal a right answer?
  • How much editing was needed before the case would be usable
  • Any output that felt inappropriate or off-target
